Final Full General Training Mock | Detecting AI Music
Section 3 is an original IELTS-style passage based on the ABC article about Spotify, AI music detection and AI disclosure.

Final General Training mock. This lesson gives Fr Jules-Davy the closest practice rhythm: everyday texts, workplace texts, then one longer general-interest text.

Exam habit: answer quickly, respect word limits, and do not spend too long on one difficult item.

Section 1 — Questions 1–14

Read the short everyday texts below and answer Questions 1–14.

Text A — Local Music Night

Riverside Hall will host a local music night on Saturday. Doors open at 6:30 pm and performances begin at 7:15 pm. Tickets are $18 online or $22 at the door. Students may buy tickets for $12 with valid identification. No food may be brought inside, but light meals and non-alcoholic drinks will be sold in the foyer.

Text B — Streaming Service Family Plan

The SoundWave Family Plan allows up to six accounts at the same residential address. Each user has their own playlists and listening history. The plan includes parental controls and offline listening. The first month is free for new customers, but a payment method must be added when joining.

Text C — Library Digital Studio

The library digital studio contains microphones, recording software and two editing computers. Bookings are limited to two hours. Users must attend a 20-minute safety introduction before their first booking. Files should be saved to a personal USB drive because computers are cleared every evening.

Text D — Sunday Train Replacement

Trains between Northlake and Central will be replaced by buses this Sunday from 5:00 am to 1:00 pm due to track maintenance. Buses leave every 15 minutes from the station car park. Allow an extra 25 minutes for travel. Standard train tickets and travel cards will be accepted on replacement buses.

Section 2 — Questions 15–27

Read the workplace texts below and answer Questions 15–27.

Text E — Digital Media Company AI Policy

Staff may use approved AI tools to assist with brainstorming, drafting routine summaries and checking grammar. AI tools must not be used to create final published material without human review. Employees must not upload confidential client information, unpublished financial data or personal staff details into public AI systems.

Where AI has contributed substantially to a public document, the team leader must decide whether a disclosure statement is required. Staff remain responsible for checking accuracy, copyright risks and tone. AI output should be treated as a draft, not as a verified source.

Text F — Audio Content Verification Procedure

Before publishing audio content, producers must confirm the source of all music, voice clips and sound effects. Content supplied by external creators must include a declaration stating whether generative AI was used. If a producer is unsure about a clip, it should be sent to the rights team before publication.

The rights team may request additional information, such as licences, written permissions or platform records. Content must not be published while ownership is unclear. If a mistake is discovered after publication, the producer must notify the editor immediately and record the issue in the corrections log.

Section 3 — Questions 28–40

Read the longer text below and answer Questions 28–40.

Making AI Music Easier to Detect

A. Spotify says it is trying to make AI-generated music easier for listeners to identify while also removing low-quality automated material from its platform. The company has a very large music catalogue and hundreds of millions of monthly users, so its decisions can influence what many people hear. It says it has removed millions of “spammy” tracks from recommendations or from the service itself.

B. The company is not banning musicians from using artificial intelligence. Instead, it says artists, songwriters and producers should be able to decide if and how AI becomes part of their creative process. However, Spotify argues that when AI is used, listeners should be told clearly so they can understand how the track was made.

C. To support this, Spotify has introduced artist verification and AI credits. Verified artists can receive a green check mark, while AI credits allow musicians to indicate whether AI was used in areas such as instrumentation or vocals. The system depends partly on artists choosing to disclose information, so some tracks may still be difficult to identify.

D. AI covers and remixes have created debate because they can become popular on social media before moving onto streaming services. In a licensed system, Spotify says the original artist should be able to choose whether their song can be remixed or covered. It also says listeners should see proper credit if such a track is made available.

E. The company has also responded to criticism about artist payments. One public claim suggested that a billion streams produced a surprisingly small payment, but Spotify argued that the total amount paid for that level of streaming is much higher. It said royalty payments can be difficult to understand because of record-company advances, multiple songwriters and the way payments are divided.

F. Spotify presents streaming as part of the music industry’s recovery after a period when piracy damaged recorded music revenue. It says its annual payments to the music industry have grown strongly since 2014. According to the company, major artists benefit, but a growing number of emerging artists are also earning more from the platform.

G. The royalty pool is calculated by stream share each month. If an artist receives a certain percentage of total listening, they receive that percentage of the pool. Spotify says fully industrial AI-generated music accounts for less than one per cent of consumption. Its position is that listeners continue to seek out authentic human music.
