Section 1 — Questions 1–13

Passage 1: A Friendship Across the World

Long before social media became common, two primary school girls began writing letters to each other through an international teacher exchange program. Saskia lived in Australia, while Heidi lived in Canada. The two girls became penpals and continued to stay in contact for more than three decades.

Their friendship began when Saskia was nine years old. At that time, communication was slow. A letter could take weeks, or sometimes months, to receive a reply. Even so, Saskia found the process exciting. She enjoyed sharing news from her own life and hearing about Heidi’s very different world.

Saskia grew up near the beach in Australia. Heidi lived on a cattle ranch in Canada, surrounded by mountains and blue lakes. To Saskia, Heidi’s life seemed almost dreamlike. She heard stories about riding horses, branding cattle and living in a landscape very different from her own.

As the years passed, their method of communication changed. Letters were gradually replaced by emails and later by social media. Although modern technology made contact easier, Saskia missed the personal feeling of handwritten letters.

After 33 years of friendship, the two women finally met face-to-face. Heidi travelled to Australia for her brother’s wedding. At first, it seemed unlikely that the meeting would happen because Saskia was also travelling overseas. However, there was one day when both women would be in the same area. They met at Thirroul Station, and Heidi later visited Saskia’s shop with her family.

For Saskia, meeting Heidi did not feel strange. She said it felt comfortable, almost as though they had met many times before.

Section 2 — Questions 14–27

Passage 2: The Value of Penpal Friendships

Penpal friendships may seem old-fashioned, but many people still value them. In the past, writing to someone overseas required patience. A person had to write a letter by hand, place it in an envelope, buy an international stamp and wait for the reply. This delay was part of the experience.

Letters also had a personal quality. People often included photos, postcards, maps, recipes, stickers, friendship bracelets or other small items. These objects made the exchange feel warm and human. A handwritten letter could become a keepsake, something to read again years later.

Some people believe penpal friendships offer emotional benefits. Writing to someone who lives far away may make it easier to speak openly. A person may share thoughts in a letter that they would find difficult to say in conversation. This can create a deep friendship, even if the two people have never met.

Penpal organisations continue to exist today. Some offer traditional letters, while others include online messaging. Their purpose is not only communication, but also connection. They allow people to learn about different cultures, develop patience and form friendships beyond their own community.

For older people, penpal writing can be especially meaningful. If travel becomes difficult, letters and messages can still bring a sense of the wider world. Some people describe it as travelling from an armchair. It can reduce loneliness and help people feel part of a larger human story.

Section 3 — Questions 28–40

Passage 3: Reading Carefully Without Overthinking

The story of long-term penpals is simple, but it contains several ideas that can easily be misunderstood in a reading test. A careless reader may assume that old forms of communication are presented as better than modern technology. However, the passage does not say this. It says only that handwritten letters have a different quality from emails and social media.

This distinction is important in IELTS Reading. A statement may sound reasonable, but it must be supported by the text. If the passage says that Saskia missed handwritten letters, this does not mean she rejected modern communication. If the passage says that letters can reduce loneliness, this does not mean they remove loneliness completely.

True / False / Not Given questions require discipline. A true answer must be directly supported by the passage. A false answer must clearly disagree with the passage. A not given answer means the passage does not provide enough information, even if the statement sounds possible.

A reflective reader may be tempted to think beyond the text. This can be dangerous in an exam. IELTS rewards evidence, not imagination. The candidate must ask, “Where does the passage say this?” If the answer cannot be found, the safest response may be Not Given.

The same skill applies to synonyms. A passage may not repeat the exact words of the question. “Stayed in contact” may mean “continued to communicate”. “Face-to-face meeting” may mean “met in person”. “Reduced loneliness” may mean “made people feel less alone”. The successful candidate learns to recognise the same meaning without inventing extra meaning.

Good exam reading is therefore balanced. The reader must be attentive, but not slow. Careful, but not anxious. Accurate, but not overly complicated. The aim is not to admire every sentence. The aim is to find the answer that the passage supports.
