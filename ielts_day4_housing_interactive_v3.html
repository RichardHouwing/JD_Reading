Section 1 — Questions 1–13

Passage 1: Saving for a Home

Free Sobolewski grew up with a strong belief that hard work and careful saving would one day lead to home ownership. Her early childhood was spent in a small, simple home in rural New South Wales. The house had only one main room, and a curtain separated the sleeping area from the rest of the home. Although the home was modest, it gave her an early sense that shelter and family stability mattered deeply.

Her parents later bought a five-bedroom house in the Brisbane suburb of Bellbowrie. Free remembered sitting on the empty dining-room floor as a child and realising that owning a home seemed possible. From that time, she wanted the same security for herself. The memory remained powerful because it connected home ownership with safety, independence and a future that could be planned.

As she grew older, Free saved every amount she could. She saved money from childhood chores, pocket money and later employment. She studied mathematics, paid off her degree and eventually gained a well-paid job with a mining company. By the age of 27, she had saved nearly $285,000. She believed this discipline would place her in a strong position when she entered the property market.

In an earlier period, such a large deposit might have been enough to buy a comfortable home. However, the housing market had changed. Free made many offers on homes near Brisbane, but they were repeatedly rejected. Some homes she tried to buy later appeared for rent, while others were purchased by investors and marked for demolition. Each unsuccessful attempt made the process feel less predictable and more discouraging.

Free felt that she had done everything she was told to do. She had studied, worked, saved and acted responsibly. Yet the connection between effort and reward no longer seemed clear. The market felt less like a fair competition and more like a hostile environment. Her experience shows how a person can follow the expected path and still find that the goal has moved further away.

Section 2 — Questions 14–27

Passage 2: Falling Prices Do Not Always Mean Affordability

Australia’s housing market has risen strongly over many years. House prices have increased by more than 400 per cent since 2000. The average home, which was once around $210,000, is now above $1 million. This long rise has changed the starting point for many buyers, especially those trying to enter the market for the first time.

Recent forecasts suggest that prices may fall by between five and 10 per cent. Some people may hear this and assume that homes will soon become affordable. However, economists argue that a small fall after decades of strong growth does not solve the deeper problem. A decline may make headlines, but it may still leave prices far beyond the reach of ordinary incomes.

One economist described the predicted fall as a market correction rather than a true improvement in affordability. She said that, for an average income to match the median home price, prices would need to fall by about 45 per cent. She did not expect such a large fall to occur. In her view, the scale of the earlier rise means that a modest fall would only partly reverse the pressure on buyers.

Some experts argue that the answer is not simply lower house prices. They believe wages need to rise faster than property prices. In other words, affordability improves when people’s incomes grow more quickly than the cost of housing. This means the relationship between wages and prices is just as important as the price of a house in isolation.

A public policy think tank has also argued that Australia needs planning reform. It supports more housing in places where people want to live. It also says there needs to be a cultural change in the way housing is viewed. A house should not only be seen as a source of wealth or investment. Its basic purpose is to provide secure housing. Without that change in thinking, the debate may continue to focus on property gains rather than human need.

Section 3 — Questions 28–40

Passage 3: Compromise and the Regional Question

For many years, one common suggestion for people priced out of the city was to move to regional areas. This advice became more popular during the pandemic, when remote work allowed more people to live away from major city centres. However, the financial advantage of moving to the regions has become much smaller. The choice is no longer as simple as leaving the city and finding a cheap alternative.

Housing data shows that the dwelling-value-to-income ratio in regional Australia is now very close to the ratio in metropolitan areas. In simple terms, homes in regional areas are not automatically cheap when local incomes are taken into account. The difference between regional and city affordability has narrowed. This means regional buyers may face pressure similar to city buyers, even when headline prices appear lower.

Free Sobolewski’s own search became a game of compromise. She attended inspections regularly and made more than 20 rejected offers over a 12-month period. As prices rose faster than she could save, she widened the area in which she was willing to buy. Her search moved further and further from central Brisbane. Each widening of the search area meant accepting a longer distance from the places and routines she had hoped to keep.

Eventually, Free had an offer accepted on a property in Redbank Plains, about 35 kilometres from the city. The home was further away than she had hoped and more expensive than she wanted. Even after buying, the financial pressure continued. Her interest rate increased, and her monthly repayments rose from about $3,800 to more than $4,200. The purchase gave her stability, but it also brought a demanding financial commitment.

Today, around half of Free’s take-home income goes to the bank. She has reduced spending where she can and lives as carefully as possible. Yet she is grateful to have a permanent place to live. She says she did not buy the house as an investment. She bought it because she wanted somewhere stable where she could build a life. For her, the house is first a home, not a strategy for making money.

Her story shows the tension in the housing debate. Falling prices may help some buyers, but they do not automatically create affordability. For many young Australians, the larger question remains whether hard work, education and responsible saving can still realistically lead to home ownership. The answer depends not only on prices, but also on wages, interest rates, supply and the expectations placed on younger buyers.
