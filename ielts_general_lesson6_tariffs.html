General Training Format Reset | Everyday, Workplace and Tariff Refund Texts
Section 3 is an original IELTS-style passage based on the ABC article about global tariff refunds after US tariffs were deemed unlawful.

General Training Reading practice. Section 1 uses everyday information, Section 2 uses workplace information, and Section 3 uses a longer general-interest text. You have 60 minutes for 40 questions.

Exam habit: read the instructions carefully, respect word limits, and answer from the text only.

Read the two workplace texts below and answer Questions 15–27.

Text E — Warehouse Induction Notice

All new warehouse employees must complete a two-hour induction before entering the loading area. The induction covers safe lifting, forklift exclusion zones, emergency exits and reporting hazards. Employees may observe the loading area from the marked visitor line before completing induction, but they must not handle stock or equipment.

Steel-capped boots and high-visibility vests must be worn at all times in the warehouse. Staff who forget protective footwear may borrow a temporary pair from reception, but only once. Repeated failure to bring required equipment will be reported to a supervisor.

Text F — Staff Training and Leave Policy

Employees are encouraged to complete at least one approved training module every six months. Online modules can be completed during quiet periods with a supervisor’s permission. External courses must be approved before enrolment if the employee wishes to claim reimbursement.

Annual leave requests should be submitted at least four weeks before the proposed start date. During December, leave is approved only after minimum staffing levels have been confirmed. Sick leave must be recorded as soon as practical, and a medical certificate is required for absences of more than two consecutive working days.

Section 3 — Questions 28–40

Read the longer text below and answer Questions 28–40.

Tariff Refunds After a Court Decision

A. Businesses around the world have begun receiving refunds for tariffs paid to United States customs after a major court decision found that a set of global tariffs had been imposed unlawfully. Economic analysis estimated that about $US80 billion had already been refunded since May, with interest included in some payments. Analysts expected that a similar amount could be returned over the following year.

B. The tariffs were introduced in April 2025 and affected importers across many countries. Australian exporters faced a flat 10 per cent tariff on goods entering the United States, while some businesses faced higher rates because their products were made in China. The sudden change created uncertainty for companies that had already sent goods overseas or had recently built systems to serve American customers.

C. One Australian business affected was Nashie, a small swimwear and garment brand. The company had set up a distribution hub to sell into the United States, and some stock was already on the water when the extra charges began. Because its garments were manufactured in China, the business faced a complicated mix of tariffs and had to pay extra money before its products could enter the country.

D. A legal challenge was brought by the Liberty Justice Centre, a conservative American not-for-profit law firm. After several rulings and appeals, the case reached the Supreme Court. The court decided that the president did not have authority to impose the broad global tariffs under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act. The decision turned a central trade policy into a large refund problem for customs authorities.

E. Even after the ruling, recovering the money was not simple. US customs authorities had to manage a very large number of claims in a short period. Nashie worked through a Californian-based customs broker and benefited from having US-linked import operations and an American bank account. Its refund took about two months to clear.

F. Other sellers faced greater difficulty. Businesses that shipped products through mail networks, including some using Australia Post, often had less direct information about the duties paid on each item. Because those payments were harder to trace, some businesses were left waiting for advice and might never receive their IEEPA tariff refunds.

G. Nashie eventually received a six-figure refund with interest. However, the company said it would not be simple to identify exactly which customer had paid which extra cost, so it would not refund individual buyers. Instead, the business planned to put the money into making a better product. Trade experts also noted that other tariffs remained in place and could lead to further legal disputes.
