Section 1 — Questions 1–13

Passage 1: Extra Charges in an Aged Care Home

When Jeff Gilling looked through the contract for the aged care home his mother Dorothy was about to enter, he noticed an extra daily charge. The fee was for additional services such as pay television, newspapers, outings and wine. These charges appeared as part of the documentation at a time when the family was already dealing with a stressful decision.

Dorothy was living with advanced dementia. Jeff believed that many of the services listed in the contract were of no practical benefit to her. She could not choose television programs or properly enjoy them. He felt she was being asked to pay for things she could not realistically use. The issue, for him, was not whether the services existed, but whether his mother could genuinely access them.

At the time, Jeff was told the extra fee was not optional. Like many families placing a loved one into aged care, he was under emotional pressure. His priority was to move his mother into safe care, not to begin a dispute about paperwork. In such circumstances, families may accept terms quickly because they are focused on immediate safety and care.

Later, Jeff investigated his mother’s rights and challenged the aged care home. He argued that the additional fees should not have been charged because his mother could not access or benefit from the services. After reviews, he received refunds totalling almost $37,000. The refund suggested that questioning the charges could make a real financial difference.

Jeff said many families may not realise they can question such charges. Others may not have the confidence, time or energy to challenge a provider. He described the process as difficult and requiring persistence. His experience shows how complex aged care paperwork can be for families who are already emotionally burdened.

Section 2 — Questions 14–27

Passage 2: Complaints, Investigations and the Law

Jeff Gilling’s experience was not isolated. Around Australia, other aged care residents, or their families, have raised concerns about being charged for services that residents could not use, did not understand, or were not able to benefit from. These concerns have drawn attention to the difference between services offered on paper and services that are meaningful for a particular resident.

In the first half of 2026, the national aged care regulator received 199 complaints concerning fees, service charges or funding connected with residential aged care. A large number of these complaints related to for-profit providers, while others concerned not-for-profit and government providers. The spread of complaints across different types of providers suggests that the issue is not limited to one part of the sector.

The regulator began investigating several providers over allegedly improper additional fees. It also said some providers had reviewed and changed their practices voluntarily. Because investigations were continuing, the regulator did not provide full details about each case. This meant the public could see the broad concern, but not every individual fact behind the investigations.

A separate class action has also been brought against one aged care provider. The claim alleges that residents were charged for extra services, including social activities and meal-related services, even when some residents could not use them because of health conditions. The provider said it would not comment on allegations while the matter was before the court. Legal proceedings of this kind can take time, especially when they involve many residents.

Aged care rules place limits on when providers can charge residents for additional services. In general, residents must agree to the services, be able to access them, and have the capacity to benefit from them. The purpose of such rules is to prevent people from being charged for services that are of no real use to them. The central question is whether a charge is connected to genuine benefit, rather than merely appearing in a contract.

Section 3 — Questions 28–40

Passage 3: Premium Services and Basic Care

Recent changes to aged care fees have allowed providers to charge residents for some premium services and lifestyle additions. These may include services beyond the ordinary standard of care. However, concerns have been raised about how these extra charges are being used. The concern is not simply that extra services exist, but that the boundary between basic care and optional additions may become unclear.

The regulator has warned that providers cannot reduce the quality of ordinary care in order to encourage residents to pay for premium options. For example, standard meals must still be nutritious, appealing and suitable for residents’ needs. A provider cannot make basic meals poorer so that residents feel pressured to purchase upgraded choices. Ordinary care must not be weakened to make paid alternatives look more attractive.

Advocates have also expressed concern about the flexibility providers have in deciding which services are treated as extras and how much they cost. If entertainment, activities or food choices are increasingly placed behind extra fees, residents with fewer financial resources may have a poorer experience than those who can pay more. This is especially sensitive because many aged care residents have limited ability to shop around or move elsewhere.

This has led to concern about a two-tier aged care system. In such a system, wealthier residents may receive a wider range of enjoyable services, while others receive only the minimum level of care. Critics argue that this would be unfair because aged care should protect dignity, not divide people according to income. The dignity of residents should not depend on whether they can afford additional lifestyle packages.

The debate shows a difficult balance. Providers may wish to offer additional services for residents who want them. However, basic care must remain adequate, respectful and available to all. Extra services should not become a way of weakening the standard support that every resident deserves. The challenge is to allow choice without allowing essential care to become second-rate for those who cannot pay more.
